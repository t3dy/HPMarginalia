The Hypnerotomachia Poliphili is a 500-page illustrated dream narrative published in Venice in 1499 — a book in which a young man named Poliphilo falls asleep and journeys through ruined classical temples, elaborate gardens, triumphal processions, and allegorical landscapes in search of his beloved, Polia. Written in a hybrid language mixing Italian, Latin, and Greek, and illustrated with 172 woodcuts of extraordinary precision, it is the most ambitious illustrated book of the fifteenth century. Read more about the book →

James Russell's 2014 PhD thesis revealed that the HP was far from an unread curiosity. He documented the marginal annotations in six copies and identified eleven distinct readers spanning two centuries: the Giovio brothers used it as a botanical encyclopedia, Ben Jonson mined it for stage design, Pope Alexander VII collected examples of verbal wit, and two anonymous alchemists independently decoded the love story as a chemistry textbook — but they disagreed about which kind of alchemy it concealed.

This site connects Russell's findings to the actual manuscript photographs, folio by folio. Browse the marginalia gallery to see annotated pages from the British Library and Siena copies.
