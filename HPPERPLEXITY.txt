Describe the 168 woodcuts in Hypnerotomachia Poliphili






The book’s 168 woodcuts form a continuous visual narrative that mirrors Poliphilo’s dream-journey through forests, ruins, gardens, rituals, and triumphal processions, with a strong emphasis on architecture and allegorical scenes. They are cut in a fine, linear style that integrates closely with the page layout and type, so that image and text read as one continuous design rather than as separate elements.

Overall character
The woodcuts “illustrate” almost every major episode: encounters with nymphs, monsters, ruins, temples, fountains, and processions, as well as emblematic objects covered in pseudo-inscriptions. Their line work is relatively spare and clear, avoiding heavy shading, which allows architectural detail and figure groupings to stand out against the white of the page.

Subject matter types
A triumphal car with a central figure on a bull in a procession, a woodcut from Hypnerotomachia Poliphili. 
Roughly, the 168 images fall into a few recurring types:

Landscapes and forests with Poliphilo wandering or meeting figures such as nymphs or monsters.

Architectural plates showing elaborate temples, pyramids, obelisks, arches, sarcophagi, and fountains.

Processional and ritual scenes, including triumphal cars, sacrificial ceremonies, and temple worship.

Mythological episodes, such as the Rape of Europa, adapted into the dream framework.

Intimate narrative moments between Poliphilo and Polia, including her kiss that revives him.

Architecture and objects
Many of the most famous cuts show impossible or fantastically composite buildings: stacked temple-pyramid-obelisk ensembles, richly ornamented doors, sculptural friezes, and fountains with hybrid creatures. Others isolate single objects—vases, sarcophagi, trophies, weathercocks, clipped trees, elaborate centerpieces—rendered almost like technical plates, with inscriptions or pseudo-hieroglyphs that invite decoding.

Figures, processions, and eros
A large subset depicts carefully staged groups: dancers, priestesses, triumphal cars pulled by animals, and crowds in ordered rows, all arranged to emphasize rhythm and symmetry. Erotic and affective moments are treated with the same formal restraint—embraces, kisses, and scenes of adoration are framed by classical interiors, checkerboard floors, and carefully balanced arches.

Style and significance
The cuts are often described as simultaneously “stark and ornate”: outlines are economical, but the compositions are packed with architectural and ornamental detail. They have been associated with the new classicizing style of late-fifteenth-century Venice and compared favorably to other incunable illustration cycles, becoming a key reference point for Renaissance book illustration and for later artists mining them for architectural and allegorical motifs.

Who created the woodcuts for the 1499 edition






The woodcuts in the 1499 Aldine edition are anonymous; no single artist can be securely named. Contemporary and modern scholars have proposed various candidates based on style, but none is universally accepted.

Attributions that have been proposed
Benedetto Bordon (Benedetto Bordone) – the Metropolitan Museum catalogue notes that the design of the woodcuts is often attributed to him, based on stylistic parallels with his known work in Venice.
​

Andrea Mantegna – some art historians have seen affinities with his severe classicizing style and architectural sense.

Gentile Bellini – also proposed due to Venetian connections and certain figure types.

Other names (Carpaccio, even a young Raphael) have occasionally been floated, but without decisive evidence.

Scholarly consensus
Most specialists today treat the blocks as the work of an unknown but highly skilled Venetian artist or workshop closely engaged with Aldus Manutius’s circle. Museums and libraries usually phrase this as “anonymous artist” or “design attributed to Benedetto Bordon/Bordone,” to reflect that the attribution remains debated rather than proven.
