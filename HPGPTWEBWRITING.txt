Home

Page title intro

The Hypnerotomachia Poliphili is one of the most enigmatic books of the Renaissance: a dream narrative, an architectural fantasia, a typographic monument, and a work that has accumulated centuries of commentary, interpretation, and readerly marks. This site is a digital scholarly platform devoted not only to the 1499 book itself, but to its afterlives in marginalia, bibliography, and modern scholarship.

It begins from a specific research problem: how to connect James Russell’s dissertation on annotated copies of the Hypnerotomachia to the manuscript and facsimile images, folio by folio, while also building a wider framework for scholars, terms, documents, and reception history. The result is part exhibition, part concordance, and part research environment.

Use the site in three ways: browse the marginalia and folio pages as a visual corpus, move through the dictionary and bibliography as a conceptual map of the field, or read the documents and code to see how the project was built and where its evidence remains provisional.

Scholars landing page

Intro block

The scholarship on the Hypnerotomachia Poliphili is wide-ranging and interdisciplinary. It spans bibliography, book history, architecture, garden studies, allegory, philology, reception history, emblematic reading, and the history of interpretation. This section organizes that scholarly landscape by person: each profile brings together the works in the collection associated with a given scholar and situates them within the larger conversation.

These pages are meant to make the field legible at a glance. They are not neutral authority pages detached from the project; they are editorially structured views over the corpus, and some biographical or classificatory fields remain review-sensitive. Where content is LLM-assisted or otherwise provisional, that status should remain visible.

Dictionary landing page

Intro block

The dictionary is the conceptual armature of the site. It defines the terms that recur across the Hypnerotomachia corpus and its scholarship—book-historical terms, annotation concepts, alchemical vocabulary, architectural and garden discourse, textual-visual rhetoric, and major historiographical debates. Its purpose is not merely lexical; it is to help readers move between page evidence and scholarly interpretation.

This section is most useful when read alongside the folio pages, essays, and bibliography. Terms are not meant to float abstractly above the project: the long-term goal is for each entry to show why the concept matters for the Hypnerotomachia specifically, what evidence in the corpus bears on it, and how it has functioned in scholarship.

About page

Intro block

This project documents the readership, marginalia, and scholarship of the Hypnerotomachia Poliphili through a static, inspectable research platform. Its immediate foundation is James Russell’s dissertation on annotated copies, but its larger ambition is to model the book as a historical and scholarly network: copies, images, signatures, annotations, scholars, bibliographic records, terms, and editorial arguments.

The site is built from a simple but explicit data flow: SQLite database to Python export scripts to static HTML, CSS, and JSON. This makes the project easier to preserve, audit, and rebuild, but it also means that evidence quality, provenance, and validation matter more than interface novelty. Where the project is confident, it says so; where it is still provisional, it should say that too.

Digital Edition page

Intro block

This page outlines the project’s eventual digital edition of the Hypnerotomachia Poliphili. It is a prospectus, not a completed edition. The goal is to show what a trustworthy edition layer would require: facsimile browsing, textual witnesses, commentary, marginalia integration, cross-links to dictionary and bibliography, and clear distinctions between what is already built, what is only partial, and what remains blocked by evidence, hosting, or review constraints.

The project already has important preconditions in place—a folio-oriented database, image catalogs, reference extraction, concordance logic, and a growing scholarly graph—but these should not be confused with a finished edition. A real edition will need stronger image validation, clearer witness logic, and a more mature editorial review process before it can claim authority.

Russell Alchemical Hands essay page

Brief orienting lead

This essay examines the subset of Russell’s research concerned with the so-called alchemical hands in annotated copies of the Hypnerotomachia. It brings together dissertation evidence, hand attribution data, and folio-level linkage in order to ask what kinds of readers these were, how their notational habits differ, and why their interventions matter for the reception history of the book. Because some of the relevant image concordance remains provisional—especially in the BL material—the essay marks uncertainty explicitly rather than smoothing it away.

Concordance Method page

Brief orienting lead

This essay explains how the project links Russell’s signature-based dissertation references to manuscript-image folders and folio pages in the site. Its purpose is methodological rather than promotional: to make the matching process visible, to distinguish secure from provisional links, and to show where future human review or multimodal validation is still needed. The concordance is one of the project’s core achievements, but it is also one of its clearest sites of epistemic caution.
