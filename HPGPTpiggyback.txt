BOUNDARY VIOLATIONS
WASTE — LLM doing what deterministic code could do:
  (none detected — the pipeline correctly uses regex and SQL for

RISK — Deterministic code doing what needs judgment:
  1. Marginal text extraction uses proximity heuristic (closest 
     single-quoted string to signature reference). This SHOULD use 
     LLM judgment to identify which quoted passage is the actual 
     annotation text vs. surrounding scholarly quotation.
     RECOMMENDATION: Add an LLM validation pass on extracted 
     marginal_text values, or flag low-confidence extractions 
     for human review.

  2. False-positive filtering uses a hardcoded English word list 
     (in, an, on, or...) to reject regex matches. This misses 
     actual quire names that happen to match common words, and 
     may over-reject valid signatures.
     RECOMMENDATION: Validate against signature_map instead of 
     word list. If a regex match IS in the map, keep it regardless 
     of word-list membership.

  3. Document metadata (author, title, year) is hardcoded in a

One type is ontology/schema prompts: these should ask only for entities, relations, constraints, and migration implications.

A second type is extraction prompts: these should ask only for parsers, confidence rules, and review queues.

A third type is interpretation prompts: summaries, scholar profiles, topic clusters, hand attributions.

You often mix all four in one instruction. That creates momentum, but it also encourages hidden shortcuts. The project got away with that because the corpus is still small. As the site grows, mixed prompts will cause more brittle pipelines.

The project is strong whenever your prompts force explicit intermediates. The most concise diagnosis is this: your vision is substantially realized as an architecture, partially realized as a corpus, and not yet fully realized as a validated scholarly system. The right next move is not broader ambition. It is converting provisional interpretive wins into durable, queryable, reviewable data structures. That is what will let the HP site become more than an impressive prototype.

The reference extraction strategy is good for recall but still brittle for precision. extract_references.py uses a regex that catches parenthesized, colon-suffixed, and bare signature references, which is correct for the dissertation, but the current approach relies on stopword filtering for false positives. That should eventually be replaced by validation against the signature map and manuscript chapter context. The Deckard critique already points in that direction.

The scholarship side is promising but still file-centric rather than database-centric. build_scholar_profiles.py groups by author out of summaries.json and writes both markdown and HTML pages, which is a practical shortcut, but it means scholar pages are currently downstream of a hand-built JSON artifact rather than normalized relational data. The moment you want review flags, multiple topic clusters, citations, glossary cross-links, or scholar-to-folio connections, that approach will start fighting you.

The bibliography code is more important than it may look. add_bibliography.py is already moving toward the right future state: a bibliography table, scholars table, scholar-work junction, and timeline events. That means the reception-history layer is beginning to exist as actual data instead of just page copy. The obvious next move is to stop letting scholar pages depend primarily on summaries.json and instead generate them from bibliography, scholars, scholar_works, and eventually doc_folio_refs.

On the dictionary tab: yes, you should add it now. It fits the site architecture extremely well. It belongs alongside Marginalia, Manuscripts, Scholarship, and About. Conceptually it bridges the book and the scholarship: users need a way to understand recurring terms such as signature, quire, folio, recto, verso, hieroglyph, ekphrasis, antiquarianism, alchemical allegory, acrostic attribution, prisca sapientia, inventio, and the names of recurring figures and motifs. That is fully consistent with the site’s stated aim to make the scholarly conversation navigable without turning the site into a search engine.

The dictionary should not just be a glossary page. It should become a reusable internal vocabulary system. In practice that means:
dictionary_terms as a table, with slug, label, definition_short, definition_long, category, language, related_terms, and review status;
a static /dictionary/ index page grouped by category;
individual /dictionary/[slug] pages;
cross-links from scholar pages, folio pages, and About pages into dictionary entries;
and tags linking each dictionary term to relevant folios, scholars, and bibliography items where possible. That directly advances your larger vision of an HP ontology rendered as a website. The ontology doc already argues that the site should expose interpretive layers, and the dictionary is one of the cleanest ways to do that.

A few concrete observations.

Your current architecture is already pointed in the right direction for a bibliography tab. The existing docs explicitly envision a /scholarship/ browser, DB-to-JSON export flow, and folio-linked scholarship rather than a detached reading list. The ontology and scholars docs also already identify the missing pieces: richer document metadata, a citations layer, and folio-level linking from scholarship into the artifact itself.

On the MIT side, the old MIT/Technical University of Delft site is valuable less as a design model than as a conceptual ancestor. Its strongest qualities are that it is book-first, chapter-indexed, durable, and includes contextual pages on typography and other editions. The chapter index exposes the whole narrative sequence, and the credits/metadata around the site make clear that it was built as a facsimile-plus-context hypertext, not as a relational scholarly system. 

That is also its main limitation. It gives access to the book, but not to the book as a network of copies, annotators, folios, scholars, and reception events. Your project can exceed it precisely because your schema is already moving toward copy-specific images, annotation hands, scholar pages, dictionary terms, and eventually folio-linked bibliography. The right move is not to imitate MIT’s interface wholesale; it is to keep MIT’s restraint and durability while adding ontology, provenance, and cross-linking.

For the bibliography tab, I would use a hybrid workflow with three layers. First, deterministic ingestion: normalize author, title, year, publication type, venue, language, DOI/ISBN/OCLC when available, and store every record with a provenance field such as source_method = manual|web_verified|llm_assisted. Second, scripted verification against authoritative bibliographic sources such as Crossref, WorldCat, library catalogs, publishers, and journal landing pages. Third, LLM assistance only for controlled tasks: resolving filename messes, reconciling variant citations, generating short annotations, and identifying probable folio/topic links for review. That keeps formatting and authority grounded while still getting the benefit of model-assisted cleanup. Your own docs are already arguing for exactly this kind of DB-backed browser rather than static hand-built pages.

On “scholarship we have missed,” a few items surfaced that look like real candidates for addition or verification. Sophia Rhizopoulou’s “On the botanical content of Hypnerotomachia Poliphili” in Botany Letters (2016) appears to be a direct HP study and would strengthen the botanical/material strand. James O’Neill’s “A Narrative in Search of an Author” is publicly visible in a 2021 journal version and should be reconciled against your current 2025 local metadata if those are separate manifestations. A recent issue of Cerae includes James Russell’s article on Pope Alexander VII’s marginalia, which looks highly relevant to your annotation/reception infrastructure. There is also a 2021 “Hypnerotomachia Poliphili and Its Afterlife” paper that may belong as lower-confidence or secondary-priority bibliography until vetted more carefully. 

I would classify bibliography growth into four queues.

Core scholarly queue: peer-reviewed books, articles, dissertations, and major edited volumes directly on HP.

Edition/translation queue: 1499 Aldine, 1545 Aldine, Jean Martin 1546 and later French reprints, Dallington 1592, Godwin 1999, and critical Italian editions.

Reception/afterlife queue: works on influence, imitation, collecting history, ownership, exhibitions, library bulletins, and public scholarship.

Peripheral but useful queue: design-history, botanical, architectural, and book-history works where HP is a substantial case study rather than the exclusive topic.

That queue structure will matter once you add the full bibliography tab, because users should be able to filter by “direct HP scholarship” versus “contextual scholarship.” Your current bibliography seed already distinguishes relevance levels; extend that aggressively instead of flattening everything into one list.
